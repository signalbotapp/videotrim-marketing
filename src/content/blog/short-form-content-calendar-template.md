The hardest part of posting consistently isn't making clips — it's deciding what
to post, when, and where, over and over, every week. A calendar removes that
decision. You set the structure once, then you fill in the blanks instead of
staring at one.

This is a working template, not a theory. Copy the tables, adjust the pillars to
your niche, and you have a month of posting mapped out in an afternoon.

## Step 1: pick a cadence you can actually sustain

The right cadence is the one you'll still be doing in three months. Consistency
beats volume — five thoughtful clips a week, every week, outperforms fifteen for
one week and then silence.

| Level | Posts per week | Best for |
|---|---|---|
| Starter | 3 | Solo creators batching around a day job |
| Steady | 5 (one per weekday) | Most growing creators — the reliable default |
| Aggressive | 7–10 | Full-time creators or teams with a backlog |

Start one level below what you think you can handle. It's far easier to add a
posting day than to recover the audience trust you lose by going quiet.

## Step 2: define your content pillars

Pillars are the three to five buckets every clip falls into. They keep your feed
coherent and stop you from posting the same kind of thing five days running.
Adjust these to your niche, but most creators land near:

- **Teach** — a tip, how-to, or framework. Drives saves.
- **Story** — a personal experience or behind-the-scenes moment. Builds trust.
- **Hot take** — an opinion or contrarian angle. Drives shares and comments.
- **Proof** — a result, case study, or before/after. Drives follows.
- **Engage** — a question, reaction, or reply to your audience. Drives comments.

The mix matters. A feed that's all "teach" feels like homework; a feed that's all
"hot take" feels exhausting. Rotate them.

## Step 4: batch one recording into a month

The calendar above works because of how it's sourced. You're not filming twenty
times — you're filming a few times and cutting many clips out of each. This is
the workflow that makes a per-weekday cadence survivable.

1. **Record one long, content-dense session.** A 30–45 minute talk, interview, or
   walkthrough holds a dozen or more clip-worthy moments. Talk in self-contained
   points so each can stand alone.
2. **Find the moments.** Transcribe it and look for the segments with a sharp
   hook, a clean arc, or a quotable line. Mark the timestamps. (Videotrim does
   this scoring for you — it ranks each segment on hook strength, story arc, and
   quotability so the best candidates surface first instead of you scrubbing the
   timeline.)
3. **Cut clean clips.** Trim each moment so it starts on the hook and ends on a
   beat — cut on the audio, never mid-word, never on silence.
4. **Reframe and caption.** Take each clip to 9:16, add word-by-word captions, and
   apply your caption preset so the batch looks consistent.
5. **Slot them into the calendar.** Assign each clip a pillar, a platform, and a
   post date. Stagger the same idea across platforms a few days apart.
6. **Schedule and forget.** Queue them, then go record next month's session.

This is the core idea behind
[turning one video into a week of content](/blog/repurpose-one-video-into-a-week/) —
one source becomes many clips, and one recording day becomes a month of posts.

## Step 5: the tracking fields to copy into your sheet

Whatever tool you use — a spreadsheet, Notion, a wall of sticky notes — these are
the columns worth tracking per clip. Copy them as your header row:

| Field | What goes in it |
|---|---|
| Hook | The first line / opening idea of the clip |
| Source timestamp | Where in the recording it came from |
| Pillar | Teach / Story / Hot take / Proof / Engage |
| Platform | TikTok / Reels / Shorts (or multiple) |
| Caption | The on-screen text or post copy |
| Post date | When it goes live |
| Status | Idea / Cut / Captioned / Scheduled / Posted |

The two fields people skip and later regret are **Hook** and **Source timestamp**.
The hook column forces you to confirm every clip actually opens strong before it's
scheduled. The timestamp column means that when a clip performs, you can go back to
the same recording and mine the moments around it — winners tend to cluster.

> A calendar isn't about creativity. It's about removing the daily decision so the
> creative part has somewhere to live.

## Making it yours

- **Niche down the pillars.** A fitness creator's pillars look nothing like a B2B
  consultant's. Keep the five-bucket structure; rename the buckets.
- **Match platforms to where your people are.** If your audience lives on one
  platform, post there daily and treat the others as overflow.
- **Review monthly, not daily.** At the end of each month, sort your sheet by
  whatever metric matters and let the winners tell you next month's pillars.
