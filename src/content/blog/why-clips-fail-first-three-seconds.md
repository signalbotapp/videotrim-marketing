A clip rarely fails because the content is bad. It fails because the first three
seconds gave the viewer a reason to leave before the content ever arrived. The
retention curve tells the whole story: a cliff in the first three seconds, then
a flat line of whoever survived it. Fix the cliff and everything downstream
improves.

Here are the six failure modes we see most often. For each: the symptom, why it
kills retention, and the fix. We're going to be a little blunt, because the
mistakes are usually obvious in hindsight.

## 1. The slow warm-up

**Symptom:** "Hey guys, so today I wanted to talk about something that's been on
my mind…" Three seconds in, you still haven't said anything.

**Why it kills:** The viewer's thumb is already moving when your clip appears.
A warm-up is a request to wait, and nobody waits. You've spent your most valuable
real estate clearing your throat.

**The fix:** Cut the warm-up entirely. The strongest sentence in your recording
is almost never the first one you spoke — it's somewhere in the middle, after
you'd hit your stride. Choose *that* as the in-point. The hello, the intro, the
"so basically" — all of it goes.

## 2. Context the viewer doesn't have

**Symptom:** The clip opens with "and that's exactly why I stopped doing it." Why
you stopped doing *what*? The viewer never heard the first half.

**Why it kills:** A clip is not a chapter. It has no before. When you start
mid-reference, the viewer feels locked out of a conversation they weren't part
of, and lockout is an instant scroll.

**The fix:** Restate the question or premise inside the clip. If the payoff is
"that's why I stopped using a to-do list," the clip needs to *contain* the setup
— either by choosing an in-point that includes it, or by leading with a caption
line that supplies it. Self-contained always beats clever.

## 3. Burying the payoff

**Symptom:** The best moment — the number, the reveal, the punchline — lands at
0:14. Everything before it is throat-clearing toward the good part.

**Why it kills:** Most viewers who leave never reach 0:14. You've protected your
best material from the people who'd have stayed for it.

**The fix:** Front-load the result, then backfill the how. "I cut my editing time
by 80% — here's the change" beats fourteen seconds of build toward the same
reveal. This is the
[anatomy of a clip](/blog/anatomy-of-a-clip-that-performs/) working in reverse:
open the loop by showing the outcome, close it by explaining it.

## 4. Dead air at the top

**Symptom:** A half-second of silence, a breath, the tail of the previous
sentence, or "ummm" before the first real word.

**Why it kills:** Dead air reads as "nothing is happening here," and the feed is
ruthless about nothing. Even a third of a second of silence at the open measurably
softens the hook. A breath is not content.

**The fix:** Cut on the audio, on the first hard syllable of the first real word
— never on the silence before it and never through the middle of a word. The clip
should begin the instant something is being said. This is fiddly to do by hand on
a waveform, which is exactly why so many clips ship with a breath at the front.

## 5. No captions on a muted feed

**Symptom:** A clip whose hook is entirely spoken, posted to a feed where
[most viewing is silent](/blog/why-captions-double-watch-time/).

**Why it kills:** If the hook lives in the audio and the audio is off, there is
no hook. The viewer sees a person's mouth moving and reads nothing, so they
assume nothing's there. You've made a great clip for headphones in a world of
mute.

**The fix:** Caption everything, word by word, in sync with speech. The first
caption line *is* the hook on a silent feed — treat it as a headline, finished
and legible within the first second. Word-level timing keeps the eye moving;
static subtitles read like a foreign film and lose people just as fast.

## 6. Starting mid-thought with no anchor

**Symptom:** The clip drops in on a sentence already underway, but unlike a
deliberate cold open, it gives the viewer nothing to grab. "...so then it
basically doubled." What doubled? Where are we?

**Why it kills:** A cold open works when the motion itself is the anchor — you
arrive to something clearly happening. It fails when you arrive to a pronoun.
Confusion isn't intrigue. Intrigue points forward; confusion points back at
something you missed.

**The fix:** Make sure the opening line contains a noun, a stake, or a visible
action — something the viewer can orient against in under a second. If the in-point
doesn't offer an anchor, move it, or supply one with the first caption line.

## The pattern under the patterns

Notice how often the fix is the same: **choose a better in-point.** Most of these
failures aren't writing problems — the good sentence is already in the recording.
They're *selection* problems. The clip starts in the wrong place.
