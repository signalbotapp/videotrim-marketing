The cleanest way to tell these two apart is to ask one question: do you already have
the clip? Zubtitle's whole flow assumes yes — you've cut the moment, and now you want
it captioned, branded, and ready for the feed. Videotrim assumes no — you have an
hour of footage and you need the clips found before anything gets captioned. Same
neighborhood, different starting line.

## Zubtitle: caption the clip you made

Zubtitle is good at the finishing stage. Drop in a short video and it adds auto
subtitles, headlines, progress bars, and resized framing for social. It's a fast way
to make a clip look intentional — the kind of polish that separates a raw screen grab
from something that reads as produced. If your pipeline already ends with "I have the
30-second cut, now make it pop," that's exactly the gap Zubtitle fills.

The thing to notice is what it expects from you upfront: a clip. Zubtitle doesn't
watch a long recording and decide which parts are worth posting. The selection — the
hard, time-consuming part — happens before you ever open it.

## Videotrim: find the clips, then caption them

Videotrim covers both steps. Give it the full recording — an MP4, a MOV, or a
YouTube or Vimeo link — and it transcribes everything, scores every segment on hook
strength, story arc, and quotability, and surfaces a ranked list of clip candidates.
You pick the ones you want instead of scrubbing a timeline.

Then it captions them. Captions are word by word, synced to the audio; edit a word
and the timing adjusts to match. There are multiple presets for font, color, and
placement, and cuts always land on the audio — never mid-word, never on a beat of
silence. Reframe vertical for Shorts, Reels, and TikTok, or wide for YouTube and
LinkedIn. One source video turns into a batch of finished clips, usually in under
five minutes.

So the contrast isn't "captions vs no captions." Both caption well. It's *what comes
before the captions.*

## Which fits your workflow

If you're already cutting clips by hand — in a phone editor, in a desktop NLE, in
whatever you trust — and the only missing step is fast, branded captions, Zubtitle
is a tidy answer. It's purpose-built for that final coat of polish and it does it
without much fuss.

If the slow part of your week is sitting through an hour of footage trying to
remember where the good line was, captioning isn't your real problem — finding is.
Videotrim handles the search and the captions in one pass, which matters most when a
single recording needs to become five or six posts. If you want to understand why
the caption quality itself moves numbers, [muted-viewing optimization](/blog/how-muted-viewing-optimization-works/)
explains what most viewers actually see.

sense if you publish steadily. If your output is lumpy — a burst after each recording,
